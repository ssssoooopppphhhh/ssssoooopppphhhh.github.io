<p>My research focuses on positive and negative feedback circuits which, due to differences in the physical properties of their components, take hold at different rates in different places. I study the feedback loops that toggle biochemical reactions on and off on the membranes of cells. Such modules can result in mesmerizing, dynamic patterns of splotches and labyrinths (2). Yet, plasma membrane protein networks are far from the only place where positive and negative feedback circuitry results in striking spatial organization.</p>

<p>In fact, the underlying mathematical architecture that drives the spontaneous emergence of such patterns is one of nature’s most ubiquitous design patterns, scaling from the molecular to the planetary. The fundamental principles of these systems (positive and negative feedback loops that act at different rates) were first proposed by Alan Turing and are known as Turing patterns (3). These patterns manifest as non-biological chemical oscillations (I highly recommend searching for videos of the Belousov-Zhabotinsky (BZ) reaction), as rhythmic bands and spots of vegetation in arid ecosystems (4, 5), and even as periodic cloud formations in our atmosphere (6).</p>

<p>My favorite example of a Turing pattern is much closer to home. And is currently yowling at me for dinner. She is a little tabby cat once described by a vet as “semi-feral and beautiful” and her mackerel-patterned coat was generated by a classic reaction-diffusion system. Research published in 2021 revealed that the interplay between activators and inhibitors of the Wnt signaling pathway pre-patterns the fetal feline epidermis, effectively drawing out a stripe map before the first hair follicle even appears (1).</p>

<p>In the keratinocytes of the embryonic tabby cat, activators such as Wnt10b and Wnt5a are secreted. Due to lipid modifications, these proteins diffuse slowly, remaining concentrated near their source and binding to Frizzled receptors on neighboring cells. These binding events trigger an intracellular, β-catenin-dependent signaling cascade that upregulates a suite of genes, including those encoding for more Wnt activators. This auto-catalysis constitutes the short-range positive feedback within the system.</p>

<p>Crucially, Wnt signaling also triggers the expression of Dkk4, a secreted inhibitor. Unlike the sticky Wnt activators, Dkk4 diffuses rapidly, spreading out into the tissue beyond the limits of the cells undergoing Wnt-mediated positive feedback. When it encounters unbound receptors, Dkk4 settles in and prevents Wnt activators from binding. This long-range negative feedback ensures that the regions characterized by Wnt signaling remain separated.</p>

<p>The resulting distinct signaling zones dictate different transcriptional programs. Regions characterized by high Dkk4 binding grow physically thicker and can be identified in the fetal epidermis even before fur growth begins and the keratinocytes in regions characterized by Wnt activation subsequently interact with melanocytes (pigment-producing cells). Ultimately, the zones of Wnt positive feedback become dark stripes, while the Dkk4-inhibited regions become the lighter background.</p>

<p>Further evidence for this mechanism is found in cats with mutations in the Dkk4 gene. These mutations alter the biophysical properties of the inhibition (such as its diffusion rate or binding affinity) leading to the diverse array of feline motifs we see in nature, from the swirled patterns of the classic tabby and the spots of the Egyptian Mau to the dramatic markings of the King Cheetah.</p>

<p>Fun notes on gene/protein names:</p>
<p>The name Frizzled comes from its discovery in Drosophila melanogaster (fruitflies). Mutant flies grew abnormal, frizzy fur.</p>
<p>Dkk stands for “dickkopf” which means “thick-head” in German. It comes from a phenotype in Xenopus laevis (African clawed frog) embryos.</p>
